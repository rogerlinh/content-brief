# Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content

Google's [automated ranking systems](https://www.google.com/search/howsearchworks/how-search-works/ranking-results/) are designed to present helpful, reliable information that's primarily created to benefit people, not to gain search engine rankings, in the top Search results. This page is designed to help creators evaluate if they're producing such content.

## Self-assess your content.

Evaluating your own content against these questions can help you gauge if the content you're making is helpful and reliable. Beyond asking yourself these questions, consider having others you trust but who are unaffiliated with your site provide an honest assessment.

Also consider an audit of the drops you may have experienced. What pages were most impacted and for what types of searches? Look closely at these to understand how they're assessed against some of the questions outlined here.

### Content and quality questions

*   Does the content provide original information, reporting, research, or analysis?
*   Does the content provide a substantial, complete, or comprehensive topic description?
*   Does the content provide insightful analysis or interesting information that is beyond the obvious?
*   If the content draws on other sources, does it avoid simply copying or rewriting those sources and instead provide substantial additional value and originality?
*   Does the main heading or page title provide a descriptive, helpful content summary?
*   Does the main heading or page title avoid exaggerating or being shocking in nature?
*   Is this the sort of page you'd want to bookmark, share with a friend, or recommend?
*   Would you expect to see this content in or referenced by a printed magazine, encyclopedia, or book?
*   Does the content provide substantial value when compared to other pages in search results?
*   Does the content have any spelling or stylistic issues?
*   Is the content produced well, or does it appear sloppy or hastily produced?
*   Is the content mass-produced by or outsourced to a large number of creators, or spread across a large network of sites, so that individual pages or sites don't get as much attention or care?

### Expertise questions


*   Does the content present information in a way that makes you want to trust it, such as clear sourcing, evidence of the expertise involved, background about the author or the site that publishes it, such as through links to an author page or a site's About page?
*   If someone researched the site producing the content, would they come away with an impression that it is well-trusted or widely recognized as an authority on its topic?
*   Is this content written or reviewed by an expert or enthusiast who demonstrably knows the topic well?
*   Does the content have any easily-verified factual errors?

## Provide a great page experience 

Google's core ranking systems look to reward content that provides a good page experience. Site owners seeking to be successful with our systems should not focus on only one or two aspects of page experience. Instead, check if you're providing an overall great page experience across many aspects. For more advice, see our page, [Understanding page experience in Google Search results](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/page-experience).

## Focus on people-first content

People-first content means content that's created primarily for people, and not to manipulate search engine rankings. How can you evaluate if you're creating people-first content? Answering yes to the questions below means you're probably on the right track with a people-first approach:

*   Do you have an existing or intended audience for your business or site that would find the content useful if they came directly to you?
*   Does your content clearly demonstrate first-hand expertise and a depth of knowledge (for example, expertise that comes from having actually used a product or service, or visiting a place)?
*   Does your site have a primary purpose or focus?
*   After reading your content, will someone leave feeling they've learned enough about a topic to help achieve their goal?
*   Will someone reading your content leave feeling like they've had a satisfying experience?

## Avoid creating search engine-first content


We recommend that you focus on creating people-first content to succeed with Google Search, rather than search engine-first content made primarily to gain rankings. Answering yes to some or all of the questions below is a warning sign that you should reevaluate how you're creating content:

*   Is the content primarily made to attract visits from search engines?
*   Are you producing lots of content on many different topics in hopes that some of it might perform well in search results?
*   Are you using extensive automation to produce content on many topics?
*   Are you mainly summarizing what others have to say without adding much value?
*   Are you writing about things simply because they seem trending and not because you'd write about them otherwise for your existing audience?
*   Does your content leave readers feeling like they need to search again to get better information from other sources?
*   Are you writing to a particular word count because you've heard or read that Google has a preferred word count? (No, we don't.)
*   Did you decide to enter some niche topic area without any real expertise, but instead mainly because you thought you'd get search traffic?
*   Does your content promise to answer a question that actually has no answer, such as suggesting there's a release date for a product, movie, or TV show when one isn't confirmed?
*   Are you changing the date of pages to make them seem fresh when the content has not substantially changed?
*   Are you adding a lot of new content or removing a lot of older content primarily because you believe it will help your search rankings overall by somehow making your site seem "fresh?" (No, it won't)

### What about SEO? Isn't that search engine-first?

You could do some things that are specifically meant to help search engines better discover and understand your content. Collectively, this is called "search engine optimization" or SEO, for short. [Google's own SEO guide](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/seo-starter-guide) covers best practices to consider. SEO can be helpful when applied to people-first content, rather than search engine-first content.

## Get to know E-E-A-T and the quality rater guidelines


Google's automated systems are designed to use [many different factors](https://www.google.com/search/howsearchworks/how-search-works/ranking-results/) to rank great content. After identifying relevant content, our systems aim to prioritize those that seem most helpful. To do this, they identify a mix of factors that can help determine which content demonstrates aspects of experience, expertise, authoritativeness, and trustworthiness, or what we call E-E-A-T.

Trust is the most important aspect of these. The others contribute to trust, but content doesn't necessarily have to demonstrate all of them. For example, some content might be helpful based on its experience, while other content might be helpful because of the expertise it shares.

While E-E-A-T itself isn't a specific ranking factor, using a mix of factors that can identify content with good E-E-A-T is useful. For example, our systems give even more weight to content that aligns with strong E-E-A-T for topics that could significantly impact people's health, financial stability, or safety or society's welfare or well-being. We call these "Your Money or Your Life" topics, or YMYL for short.

[Search quality raters](https://www.google.com/search/howsearchworks/how-search-works/rigorous-testing/) are people who give us insights on if our algorithms seem to be providing good results, a way to help confirm our changes are working well. In particular, raters are trained to understand if content has strong E-E-A-T. The criteria they use to do this is outlined in our [search quality rater guidelines](https://services.google.com/fh/files/misc/hsw-sqrg.pdf).

Search raters have no control over how pages rank, so their data is not used directly in our ranking algorithms. Rather, we use them like a restaurant might get feedback cards from diners. The feedback helps us know if our systems seem to be working.

Reading the guidelines may help you self-assess how your content is doing from an E-E-A-T perspective, consider improvements, and help align it conceptually with the different signals that our automated systems use to rank content.

## Ask "Who, How, and Why" about your content


Consider evaluating your content in terms of "Who, How, and Why" to stay on course with what our systems seek to reward.

### Who (created the content)

Something that helps people intuitively understand the E-E-A-T of content is when it's clear who created it. That's the **"Who"** to consider. When creating content, here are some who-related questions to ask yourself:

*   Is it self-evident to your visitors who authored your content?
*   Do pages carry a byline, where one might be expected?
*   Do bylines lead to further information about the author or authors involved, giving background about them and the areas they write about?

If you clearly indicate who created the content, you're likely aligned with the concepts of E-E-A-T and on a path to success. We strongly encourage adding accurate authorship information, such as bylines, to content where readers might expect it.

### How (the content was created)


It's helpful to readers to know how a piece of content was produced: this is the **"How"** to consider including in your content.

For example, product reviews can build trust with readers when they understand the number of products tested, the test results, and how the tests were conducted, all accompanied by evidence of the work involved, such as photographs. We share more about this advice in our [Write high-quality product reviews](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/specialty/ecommerce/write-high-quality-reviews) help page.

Many types of content may have a "how " component. That can include automated, AI-generated, and AI-assisted content. Sharing details about the processes involved can help readers and visitors better understand any unique and useful role automation may have served.

If automation is used to generate content substantially, here are some questions to ask yourself:

*   Is the use of automation, including AI-generation, self-evident to visitors through disclosures or in other ways?
*   Are you providing background about how automation or AI-generation was used to create content?
*   Are you explaining why automation or AI was seen as useful to produce content?

Overall, AI or automation disclosures are useful for content where someone might think "How was this created?" Consider adding these when it would be reasonably expected. For more, see our blog post and FAQ: [How Google Search views AI-generated content](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2023/02/google-search-and-ai-content).

### Why (was the content created)


**"Why"** is perhaps the most important question to answer about your content. Why is it being created in the first place?

The "why" should be that you're creating content primarily to help people, content that is useful to visitors if they come to your site directly. If you're doing this, you're aligning with E-E-A-T generally and what our [core ranking systems](https://developers.google.com/search/updates/core-updates) seek to reward.

If the "why" is that you're primarily making content to attract search engine visits, that's not aligned with what our systems seek to reward. If you use automation, including AI-generation, to produce content for the primary purpose of manipulating search rankings, that's a [violation of our spam policies](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials/spam-policies#scaled-content).

## The Semantic SEO Content checklist:

1.  Prioritize Proper Word Sequence: Ensure sentence structure emphasizes key attributes and context early for clarity to users and search engines, aiding relevance configuration.  
      
    Example: Instead of "A car, which is red and fast, sped by," write "A fast, red car sped by."
    
2.  Prioritize Factual Certainty: Convey information with precision and confidence, avoiding opinion-based words (e.g., "will," "should") to build trust and authority. Focus on verifiable facts.  
      
    Example: Instead of "This strategy should boost your traffic," write "This strategy is designed to boost traffic by targeting relevant keywords."  
      
    
3.  Eliminate Fluff and Redundancy: Remove unnecessary words or phrases that don't add value, ensuring content is direct, clear, and focused on user queries.  
      
    Example: Instead of "It is an undeniable fact that in order to achieve success, one must work hard," write "Hard work is essential for success."  
      
    
4.  Incorporate Numeric Values: Use precise numbers and data (e.g., quantities, percentages, statistics) to enhance credibility, specificity, and scannability.  
      
    Example: Instead of "Our product has many features," write "Our product offers 7 key features, including real-time analytics."  
      
    
5.  Qualify Instances with Detail: Add descriptive details (qualifiers like number, severity, type) to instances to provide specific, comprehensive, and authoritative information.  
      
    Example: Instead of "The disease has symptoms," write "The disease presents with 3 common symptoms: fever, cough, and fatigue."  
      
    
6.  Understand and Use Contextual Verbs: Choose verbs that accurately reflect the specific context (e.g., health, skill development, improvement) to align with user intent and enhance semantic relevance.  
      
    Example: For health, use "boosts" (e.g., "Vitamin C boosts immunity"). For skills, use "develops" (e.g., "Practice develops proficiency").  
      
    
7.  Provide Examples After Plural Nouns: Follow plural nouns with specific examples to clarify meaning, enhance understanding, and improve content relevance.  
      
    Example: Instead of "There are various marketing tools," write "There are various marketing tools, such as Google Analytics, SEMrush, and Ahrefs."  
      
    
8.  Maintain Parallel Structure in Lists: Use consistent grammatical forms (e.g., all verbs, all nouns, all adjectives) for items in a list to improve readability and coherence.  
      
    Example: Consistent (verbs): "To succeed, you need to plan, execute, and review." Inconsistent: "To succeed, you need planning, to execute, and reviewing."  
      
    
9.  Optimize First Sentence of Subordinate Text: Ensure the first sentence under a heading directly answers or addresses the heading's topic for immediate clarity and engagement.  
      
    Example: Heading: "Benefits of Regular Exercise." Optimized first sentence: "Regular exercise offers numerous benefits, including improved cardiovascular health, weight management, and stress reduction."  
      
    
10.  Structure Conditional "If" Statements Clearly: Present the main statement or declaration before the conditional "if" clause to enhance clarity and emphasize the primary point.  
      
    Example: Instead of "If you want to improve your writing, you should practice daily," write "You should practice daily if you want to improve your writing."  
      
    
11.  Match Anchor Text with Target Page Title: Ensure hyperlink anchor text closely reflects the title of the linked web page for strong contextual relevance and better user experience.  
      
    Example: If linking to a page titled "Advanced SEO Techniques for 2025," use anchor text like "Advanced SEO Techniques for 2025" or "learn about Advanced SEO Techniques for 2025."  
      
    
12.  Use "You/Your" to Address the Reader: Focus on the reader by using "you" and "your," and limit the use of "I" or "we" to make content more direct and personal.  
      
    Example: "You will find these strategies effective for your goals," not "We believe these strategies are effective."  
      
    
13.  Ensure Logical Content Flow and Connectivity: Maintain a continuous, logical connection between paragraphs and sections. Ensure the introduction and conclusion are thematically linked (e.g., problem/solution).  
    Example: An article introduces the challenge of plastic pollution and concludes by summarizing actionable solutions discussed throughout the text.  
      
    
14.  Use Simple Present Tense for General Truths: Describe facts, general truths, and habitual actions using the simple present tense for clarity and authoritativeness.  
      
    Example: "Semantic SEO focuses on meaning and context," not "Semantic SEO will focus on meaning and context."  
      
    
15.  Write Concisely and Clearly: Use short, direct sentences. Eliminate wordiness but ensure all necessary information for the topic is comprehensively covered.  
      
    Example: Instead of "The reason for the delay was due to the fact that there was unexpected heavy traffic," write "The delay was due to unexpected heavy traffic."  
      
    
16.  Avoid Personal Opinions and Informal Language: Present information objectively. Do not use subjective opinions, colloquialisms, or speculative language. Maintain consistency across all site content.  
      
    Example: State "Data suggests a 20% increase in user engagement," not "I feel like users are probably more engaged now."  
      
    
17.  Employ Positive or Neutral Language: Choose vocabulary carefully, especially verbs. Generally, use words with positive connotations. Use negative terms only for necessary warnings or to describe adverse impacts, and not within a positive framing.  
      
    Example: "This approach optimizes resource allocation," rather than "This approach doesn't waste as many resources."  
      
    
18.  Use Precise and Exact Data (Especially for YMYL Topics): Provide exact figures and data points, avoiding approximations, particularly for Your Money or Your Life (YMYL) content.  
    Example: "The medication contains 5.25mg of active ingredient per dose," not "The medication contains about 5mg of active ingredient."  
      
    
19.  Be Detailed Yet Concise with Specifics: Provide specific details concisely. For instance, specify time ranges instead of general periods.  
      
    Example: Instead of "The offer is valid for a short time," write "The offer is valid from May 15th to May 20th."  
      
    
20.  Ensure Consistent Word Order in Q&A: Maintain parallel structure and word order for entities (nouns, verbs, adjectives) between a question and its corresponding answer.  
      
    Example: Q: "What are the primary advantages of solar power?" A: "The primary advantages of solar power are its renewability, cost-effectiveness, and low environmental impact."  
      
    
21.  Use Colons (:) to Introduce Explanations or Lists: A colon should signal that the following text completes, explains, or lists elements related to the preceding clause.  
      
    Example: "The project has three key phases: planning, execution, and review."  
      
    
22.  Provide Multiple Relevant Measurement Units: Where applicable, use more than one unit of measurement (e.g., imperial and metric), prioritizing units common for the target audience.  
      
    Example: "The recommended daily water intake is 8 glasses (approximately 2 liters)."  
      
    
23.  Prioritize Detailed and Specific Numbers: Always opt for precise figures over general statements when data is available.  
      
    Example: "The customer satisfaction rate increased by 15% last quarter," not "Customer satisfaction has improved significantly."  
      
    
24.  Avoid Starting Paragraphs with Direct Quotations: Introduce a quotation within a sentence rather than beginning a paragraph with it.  
      
    Example: "Experts emphasize the importance of user experience, with one stating, 'The user always comes first.' This principle guides our design."  
      
    
25.  Include at Least Three Items in In-Paragraph Lists/Examples: When listing items or providing examples within a sentence or paragraph, aim for a minimum of three elements for richness.  
      
    Example: "Effective content marketing involves creating valuable blog posts, engaging social media updates, and informative case studies."  
      
    
26.  Use "and" or "or" for Entities in Headings/Descriptions: Connect two distinct entities in headings or meta descriptions using "and" or "or," not a slash ("/").  
      
    Example: Heading: "Comparing SEO and SEM Strategies," not "SEO/SEM Strategies."  
      
    
27.  Explain Localized Dish Names in Descriptions: For food items with specific regional or cultural names, provide an explanation or description in the body text, not directly in the main title if it hinders clarity for a broader audience.  
      
    Example: Title: "Banh Mi." Description: "Banh Mi, a popular Vietnamese sandwich, typically consists of a baguette filled with savory meats, pickled vegetables, and fresh herbs."  
      
    
28.  Thoroughly Proofread All Content: Before publishing, meticulously check for and correct any errors in spelling, grammar, punctuation, and formatting.  
      
    Example: Use grammar/spell-checking tools and perform a manual read-through to catch any mistakes.  
      
    
29.  Create Specific and Semantic Image Descriptions (Alt Text): Ensure image alt text is highly descriptive, contextually relevant (semantic), and accurately portrays the image content for accessibility and SEO.  
      
    Example: Alt text for an image of a golden retriever playing fetch: "Golden retriever dog joyfully catching a red ball in a sunny park," not just "dog playing."  
      
    
30.  Differentiate Factual Statements from Possibilities: Use present tense for established facts or consensus. Use modal verbs like "can," "could," or "may" to indicate possibilities, suggestions, or potential outcomes.  
      
    Example (Fact): "The Earth is the third planet from the Sun." Example (Possibility): "Implementing these changes may lead to improved performance."  
      
    
31.  Capitalize First Letter of Each Word in All Headings.  
      
    Example: For a section about SEO, use "What Is Search Engine Optimization?" not "What is search engine optimization?".
    
32.  For "How-To" Articles, Avoid Definition Headings for the Core Entity.  
      
    Example: In an article "How to Bake a Cake," start with steps, not a heading "What is a Cake?".  
      
    
33.  Ensure Headings are Not Redundant and Align with the Customer Journey.  
      
    Example: If a previous section covered "Beginner Tips for Gardening," the next shouldn't be "More Beginner Tips" but perhaps "Intermediate Gardening Techniques" if that matches user progression.  
      
    
34.  In FAQs/Featured Snippets, Bold the Direct Answer, Not Just SEO Keywords.  
      
    Example: Q: "What is the capital of France?" A: "Paris is the capital of France." (Bold "Paris").  
      
    
35.  Maintain Consistent Font Style & Size for Body Text.  
      
    Example: Use Arial size 11 consistently for all main paragraph text, excluding headings.  
      
    
36.  Format Paragraphs to 2-3 Lines with Adequate Spacing Between Them.  
      
    Example: Keep paragraphs short for readability and ensure a clear visual break (e.g., one empty line) between each.  
      
    
37.  Ensure Grammatically Perfect, Complete Sentences (Subject-Verb), and No Plagiarism.  
      
    Example: Always write "The cat sleeps" (complete) not "Cat sleeps" (incomplete). Use plagiarism checkers before publishing.  
      
    
38.  Adhere Strictly to Capitalization, Punctuation, and Spacing Rules; Use Bold/Italics Sparingly and Purposefully.  
      
    Example: Use bold for strong emphasis on a key phrase once, not multiple times in a paragraph, to avoid reader fatigue.  
      
    
39.  Use Bullet Points (e.g., •) for Lists within Content; Avoid Complex Numbering (e.g., 1.1, 1.1.2).  
      
    Example:
    

*   Item 1
    
*   Item 2
    
*   Item 3 Not: 1.1 Item 1 1.1.1 Sub-item  
      
    

41.  Use an Oxford Comma (Serial Comma) Before "and" or "or" in a List of Three or More Items within a Sentence.  
      
    Example: "Key elements include research, writing, and editing." (comma before 'and').  
      
    
42.  Avoid Fallacies and Ensure No Contradictions Within the Article or Across the Entire Website.  
      
    Example: If one article states X is beneficial, another article on the same site should not state X is harmful without clear contextual differentiation and explanation.  
      
    
43.  Understand Reply Modalities: Use present tense for facts (consensus); "can/could/may" for research-based statements; "should" for personal experience/suggestions. Match question's modality. Declare answer before citing. Use period for decimals (e.g., 3.7), not comma (e.g., 3,7) in English numerical data.  
      
    Example (Fact): "Water boils at 100°C." Example (Research): "Studies suggest this diet may improve health (Author, 2023)." Example (Suggestion): "You should consider these factors for your project."  
      
    
44.  Employ Relevant Entities and Semantically Related Words: Use contextually appropriate, unique terms and combinations (like experts do) to optimize microsemantics and demonstrate Topical Authority.  
      
    Example: Instead of just "good phone," use terms like "flagship smartphone with advanced computational photography capabilities" if discussing high-end mobile cameras in a tech review.  
      
    
45.  When providing examples after plural nouns, list at least three distinct and relevant items if appropriate and available, to enhance specificity.  
      
    Example: "Effective SEO strategies include keyword research, on-page optimization, and quality link building."

## KORAY 12 WRITING RULES (BẮT BUỘC ÁP DỤNG TRONG MỌI BRIEF)

### R1: Proper Word Sequence
Viết attribute quan trọng lên đầu câu. Ngữ cảnh quyết định nghĩa.
- ❌ "Trong xây dựng kết cấu, thép thanh vằn được dùng phổ biến"
- ✅ "Thép thanh vằn được dùng phổ biến trong xây dựng kết cấu cầu, nhà xưởng, cơ sở hạ tầng"

### R2: Factual Certainty
Tránh "sẽ", "nên", "có thể". Dùng present tense, definite language.
- ❌ "Thép này sẽ bền theo thời gian"
- ✅ "Thép thanh vằn được thiết kế cho tuổi thọ 50+ năm trong môi trường biển"

### R3: Eliminate Fluff
- ❌ "Đây là điều không thể phủ nhận rằng để đạt thành công"
- ✅ "Kiên trì là chìa khóa thành công trong xây dựng kết cấu thép"

### R4: Numeric Values
Luôn dùng số cụ dụng: MPa, mm, kg, %, JIS, ASTM, TCVN — không "nhiều", "lớn".
- ❌ "Thép này có nhiều ưu điểm vượt trội"
- ✅ "Thép này có 5 ưu điểm: bền cao (490 MPa), chống gỉ, nhẹ hơn 30%, dễ gia công, giá cạnh tranh"

### R5: Qualify Instances
Chỉ rõ mức độ, loại, số lượng.
- ❌ "Các tiêu chuẩn thép phổ biến"
- ✅ "Các tiêu chuẩn phổ biến: JIS G3506 (Nhật Bản), TCVN 1651 (Việt Nam), ASTM A36 (Mỹ)"

### R6: Contextual Verbs
Dùng động từ đúng ngữ cảnh ngành:
- Kỹ thuật xây dựng → "thiết kế cho", "chịu lực", "chịu kéo", "chịu ăn mòn"
- Kinh doanh/phân phối → "cung cấp", "phân phối", "báo giá"

### R7: Examples After Plural Nouns
Sau danh từ số nhiều phải có ít nhất 3 ví dụ cụ thể.

### R8: Parallel Structure in Lists
Trong danh sách: cùng loại từ (tất cả là danh từ HOẶC tất cả là động từ.

### R9: Optimize First Sentence Under Heading
Câu đầu tiên phải trả lời trực tiếp heading. Không có prelude.

### R10: Answer Directness
Trả lời ngay. Không có "Trong bài viết này", "Chúng ta sẽ tìm hiểu"

### R11: Anchor Text Context
Trước internal link: nhắc keyword 1-5 lần với ngữ cảnh rõ ràng.

### R12: Do Not Delay Answer — Bold câu trả lời chính, không phải keyword trong FAQ.

```
[MAIN] Định nghĩa / Tổng quan
[MAIN] Đặc điểm kỹ thuật / Phân loại
[MAIN] Ứng dụng / So sánh

[SUPP] Kết luận / Lưu ý / Antonym Ending
```

**Tỷ lệ:** [MAIN] = 65-80% | [SUPP] = 20-35% tổng số H2

Định nghĩa → Thuộc tính → Ứng dụng → Brand context tự nhiên.
